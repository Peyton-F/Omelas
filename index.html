About the City

A people without guilt,
a place without need.

Omelas sits between green mountains and a bright sea, where summer keeps its long promise and the festival bells begin at dawn.

The city is old, though no record states how old. Its founding is celebrated each summer, but the year is never given, and no citizen has thought to ask. What is remembered is that Omelas was built to be happy. Not merely tolerable, not merely prosperous, but joyful in the way that children are joyful before they learn the names of unkind things.

Visitors are sometimes surprised to find that the people of Omelas are not simple. They are mature, intelligent, passionate, and complex. They know hardship in the sense that the seasons turn and grain must be tended and love is sometimes lost. What they do not know is the dull weight that follows a life in our own cities: the small daily fear, the suspicion that the world is rigged against them. In Omelas it is not.

There is no king in Omelas. There are no priests of the punishing kind. No standing army patrols the walls. The libraries are large, the markets honest, the gardens public. Children grow tall in good light. The old die without indignity.

We have lost the habit of believing in such a place. That is our problem, not Omelas'.

The newcomer may find the brightness of Omelas hard to credit. There is an instinct, especially in the traveler, to look for the catch: the secret tax, the unmentioned tyrant, the dark room. We invite the skeptical visitor to walk the streets, attend the festival, share a meal, and form their own view.

The Festival of Summer

The whole city is singing.

Each year, on the longest day, the bells of Omelas ring at dawn and the festival begins. It is the city's truest face, what every other day of the year is quietly aimed toward.

The morning is for the procession. Children in white run ahead of the horses, who have not been broken and who wear silver bells in their manes. Old men play flutes in the doorways. By midday the markets are unattended; everyone is out.

The afternoon is for the games on the meadow north of the city. There are races, music competitions, dances that have no name and no rules. The old sit beneath the oaks and drink the dark wine that comes from the south slopes. No one keeps score.

The evening is for the great fire in the central plaza, and the singing, which lasts until the stars are out. There are no speeches. There is no flag-lowering. The festival ends when the people are tired, and not before.

Living in Omelas

A life that is, in every way, well-arranged.

There is no shortage in Omelas. Not of food, not of housing, not of attention, not of time. Citizens describe the texture of daily life here in terms outsiders find difficult to credit.

01 / Housing

A home for every family.

The city's housing is held collectively and assigned by need and choice. No one in Omelas has ever been turned out for inability to pay. The waiting list for the harbor district is admittedly long, but new construction in the eastern slopes opens each spring.

02 / Schools

Education without scarcity.

Children in Omelas learn at their own pace, with their own teachers, in groups they themselves help to form. There are no examinations. Literacy is universal by age seven. The university is open to all and free of cost.

03 / Health

Care that asks no fee.

The physicians of Omelas attend to anyone who calls for them. Childbirth is supported, the dying are companioned, and the long middle of life (that part our cities ignore) is held by the city's network of neighborhood houses.

04 / Work

Labor without humiliation.

People in Omelas work. The fields are tended, the bakeries run before dawn, the libraries are kept. But no citizen labors at a task they have not chosen, nor under conditions they have not consented to. The rotation system is the city's oldest institution.

05 / Justice

Disputes settled in daylight.

What disputes arise are heard by panels of neighbors, in open session. Punishment is rare and gentle. The last serious crime in Omelas is so far in the past that its details have softened into folklore.

06 / Death

The end without fear.

The old of Omelas die at home, attended, in good light. Their dying is not hurried and not delayed. The city remembers them by name for as long as anyone living wishes to speak the name.

Frequently Asked

The questions visitors most often ask.

Is Omelas really crime-free?

Yes. The city's records, which are open to any citizen or visitor, show no reported violent crime in the last forty years. Minor disputes are settled by neighborhood panels and rarely require formal intervention. We acknowledge this strains belief, particularly for those arriving from cities where crime is treated as inevitable. We can only invite you to walk the streets and form your own view.

How is everyone in Omelas so happy?

Happiness in Omelas is not a mood imposed on the citizens; it is a condition that follows from the way the city is arranged. When people are housed, fed, taught, attended to in illness, and free to choose their work, the daily friction that grinds down lives elsewhere is simply absent here. The result is not euphoria. It is something steadier: the absence of the dull weight.

Where does the city's wealth come from?

Omelas is not a wealthy city in the sense the question usually means. We do not extract from other places. The land is generous, the sea is generous, and the citizens have arranged their labor so that no one's surplus is taken to support another's idleness. What looks like wealth from outside is, more accurately, the absence of waste.

Are there laws in Omelas? A government?

There are agreements, kept by the citizens themselves and revised when needed in open assembly. There is no monarch, no senate, no standing court. The few offices that exist (the keeper of the libraries, the convener of the spring assembly, the warden of the festival) are rotated by lot and held briefly. The city is governed, but it is not ruled.

Why do some people leave Omelas?

A small number of citizens, each year, leave the city. They do not return, and they do not say where they go. It is a sorrow, but a familiar one. We do not stop them. We do not follow them. We honor their leaving as we honor the choice of any citizen to choose their own life. the reasons are private to each. read more

Can I move to Omelas?

Visitors are welcome year-round. Those who wish to stay longer are invited to do so. The transition from visitor to citizen has no formal step. At some point you simply realize you have stayed, and the city realizes it too. There is no application, no fee, no oath. There is only the question, asked of yourself, whether you are willing to live here in full knowledge of how the city is arranged.

Hi, I'm Peyton. I made this. Omelas is a real city in fiction, and this is my attempt at giving it a website.

I'm a mechanical engineering student studying in California, with one foot in CAD software and the other in code. When I'm not modeling gear trains or wiring up Arduinos, I'm usually building software tools for whatever problem won't leave me alone that week. If you want the longer version, my personal site has more about me and what I'm currently working on.

This site is a creative adaptation of Ursula K. Le Guin's short story "The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas." It started as a question: what would the city's own promotional website look like, if it had one? Bright, welcoming, full of testimonials and FAQs, the kind of thing you'd land on while planning a trip. And then, somewhere on it, the truth, exactly where Le Guin places it in the story, halfway through, after you've already fallen for the place.

Every visual is hand-coded SVG. The ambient music is generated live in the browser with the Web Audio API, with the chord progression rewriting itself when you find the room. There are no images on this site. There is no library doing the heavy lifting. It is all just HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, the way Omelas itself is just a town.

there is one more thing you should know about your city

In a basement under one of the beautiful public buildings of Omelas, in a locked room with no window, a child is kept.

The room is small. Three paces by two. The walls are stone and damp, even in the dryness of summer. A single bulb burns somewhere out of reach, or perhaps there is no bulb and only the small slot at the bottom of the door admits the light. The child does not know.

The child is perhaps six. It looks younger because it is so undernourished. It was not born in the room, but it has been there long enough that it has forgotten the time before. It remembers, sometimes, the feel of sunlight on its face. It remembers a voice saying its name. It does not remember the name.

It sits in the corner farthest from the door, where the mop is kept. The mop is the thing it sees most often. Twice a day, someone opens the door and sets a half-bowl of cornmeal and grease on the floor. The someone does not speak. The someone does not look at the child. The door closes.

The child is the price.

Every citizen of Omelas knows it is there. They are told when they are old enough to understand, sometimes at eight, sometimes at twelve, sometimes later if a parent cannot bring themselves to say it. They are shown the door if they ask. Some go in. Most do not.

The terms are these: the wealth of the city, the kindness of its people, the long summers, the fact that no one in Omelas dies hungry or afraid, all of it depends on the misery of this one child. If the child were comforted, if it were washed, if it were given a kind word, in that same hour everything good in Omelas would begin to come undone. The orchards would wither. The festival would sour. The bells would crack. This is known to be true. It is not a rumor. The trade is exact and the trade is known.

You have walked the bright streets. You have read the testimonials. You have considered the schools, the harbor, the libraries. You have understood the terms now. The city is real and the price is real and you have learned both.

You leave Omelas. You walk out of the gates and you keep walking.

The road goes through the orchards, and then through the fields, and then into country that is not the city's. The light dims. The bells, when they sound at sundown, are far behind you and do not reach where you are.

You do not know where you are going. The people who walk away from Omelas never seem to know. Only that they cannot stay, having seen, and so they go on, into the dark.
